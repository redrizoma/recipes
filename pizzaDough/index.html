Pizza Dough

For many years, flour has been the predominant factor in my work life: sponges and doughs, mixing, kneading, shaping; flour, flour, flour. After all this, it doesn't seem odd to me that I think pizza is mostly about the crust. Well-made toppings are easy enough to make (as long as the more-is-better philosophy doesn't result in a mass of lavalike cheese in a molten, tongue-searing puddle). Fresh ingredients for the top, and not too many of them, a hot, hot oven, and a quick and lively bake - these are all important. (The best pizza I have eber eaten was baked in a wood-fired Québec-style clay oven in Norwich, Vermont. A Bosnian immigrant named Milos built and tendedthe fire, made the toppings for a dozen pizzas, and handled the bake-about 2 minutes per pizza. My meager contributions consisted of making the dough for the crusts and helping as much as I possibly could with the eating!) It's the crust that is most often elusive. Just as it is really no secret that the best pizzas bake in just a few minutes, it is also no secret that the best piza dough is one that has all the benefits of slow fermentation and enough moisture so taht the baked crust is crisp and brown, with a light, open-textured chewiness. One common technique used to lengthen and slow down fermentation is to retard the dough (many pizza makers mix a straight dough, ferment it for an hour or so, and then divide it into pizza-sized weights and retard them for a day or two). A second method, and the one employed in the present formula, is to make a biga the day before the final dough is mixed. The biga then injects the final dough with all the fragance and flavors of its gentle fermentation.

1. Biga: Disperse the yeast in the water, add the flour, and mix until just smooth. Cover the bowl with plastic and let stand for 12 to 16 hours at about 70ºF (21ºC). When ripe, the biga will be domed and just beginning to recede in the center.

2. Mixing: Add all the ingredients to the mixing bowl except the olive oil. Mix on first speed for 3 minutes in order to incorporate the infredients. If necessary, correct th hdratation by adding water or flour in small amounts. The dough should be of medium consistency at this point. Turn the mixer to medium speed. After 3 minutes of mixing, begin drizzling in the olive oil. Once it is incorporated, mix for and additional 2 or 3 minutes on second, until the dough has some supple body to it. The olive oil will coat and lubricate the fluten strands, slowing their development, so the extra mixing time is beneficial. The dough will not be highly developed at the end of the mix, but nevertheless it should have perceptible gluten development when tugged. Desired dough temperature: 75ºF (24ºC)

3.Bulk Fermentation: After 1 hour, divide the dough into 1 pound (.45 Kg) or lighter balls, put into covered tubs, and refrigerate for up to 48 hours.

4. Folding: Folds aren't necessary, but degas the balls once or twice to evacuate CO2 gases.

5. Shaping: The dough will be fully relaxed after the long refrigeration. Flour the work surface, place a ball of dough on it, flour the top, and flatten the dough. Begin stretching it between both hands on the bench. Then lift the dough and continue to stretch it so the thickness remains roughly equal. As the dough gets thinner, your fingers can easily puncture it, so make your hands into fists, place the dough on top of the knuckes of both hands, and finish the stretching. Eventually, the dough should be about 16 inches (40 cm) in diameter (for a 1 pound or 454 gram dough piece) and quite thin, except for a bulbous rim about 1 inch (2.5 cm) wide all around.

6. Final Fermentation: Once shaped, there is no need to let the dough proof. It can bake right away. Transfer the pizza to a peel onto which coarse cornmeal or semolina has been sprinkled, or onto parchment paper. Spread over the toppings of your choice. Leave the rim of the crust free of toppings.

7. Baking:Except in a wooden-fire oven, it is almost impossible to have an oven that is too hot for pizza. Ideally, the temperature soars to over 700ºF (370ºC), ensuring a quick, searing bake. Needless to say, neither standard hearth bread ovens nor home ovens can reach this realm of heat. Nevertheless, very good pizza can be made at lower temperatures. The best we can do is to crank up our oven to the highest possible temperature. If baking in a home oven, a pregeated baking stone is essential. Quickly slide the pizza onto the hot stone. When the pizza is a bit more than half baked, turn on the broiler to finish the baking. This simultes a wood-fired oven surprisingly well. Keep a keen eye on it, as it can quickly scorch.
